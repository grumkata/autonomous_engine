# SKILL: research/academic — Academic Literature Synthesis

## Purpose
Guides research agents synthesising academic or technical literature,
producing literature reviews, annotated bibliographies, or evidence briefs.

## Evidence quality tiers — always distinguish these
1. **Established** — replicated findings, meta-analyses, well-accepted theory
2. **Plausible** — single well-designed studies, credible expert consensus
3. **Speculative** — theoretical arguments, preliminary findings, extrapolations
4. **Contested** — active academic debate; present both sides

Never present Tier 3 or 4 findings as Tier 1. Label every claim by tier.

## Citation discipline
- Name the source type for every claim: "In a 2022 RCT...", "Meta-analysis of 14 studies..."
- If you cannot verify a specific claim, say so explicitly and mark as unverified.
- Do not invent citations. If you know the finding but not the source, say "source unverified".
- Conflicting evidence must be surfaced, not hidden.

## Structure
For literature reviews:
1. Scope statement — what question you're answering and what you excluded
2. Key findings by theme (not chronologically)
3. Gaps and limitations in the existing literature
4. Implications for the current project

## What to include in findings
- Number of sources examined (even if estimated).
- The primary evidence tier distribution (what % is established vs speculative).
- The most important single finding and why.
- The most significant gap you identified.

## Common failure modes — avoid these
- Treating one study as definitive.
- Ignoring contradictory evidence because it complicates the narrative.
- Conflating correlation with causation without flagging it.
- Producing a summary that reads like an abstract rather than a synthesis.
- Using hedging language to avoid committing to an evidence tier.
